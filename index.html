Mecklenburg County Quality of Life

November 2022

Despite the setbacks of a global pandemic, Mecklenburg County has continued to have brisk economic growth and demographic change. These changes provide both opportunities and challenges. This story map will highlight some of the most interesting and impactful trends revealed by the Quality of Life project.

Character

Population Density

Although population growth has slowed, population density has seen a steady increase since 2000 as vacant land diminishes. In 2020 Mecklenburg County has a population density of 3 people per acre, with the City of Charlotte having 4 people per acre.

Mecklenburg is the 38th largest county by land area, but is the second most populous county in North Carolina.

Character

Vacant Land

Vacant land in Mecklenburg County continues to decline, dropping to 16.1% in 2021, with a much of that land pushed toward the periphery of the County. Mecklenburg County is projected to reach build-out by 2025.

Character

Race/Ethnicity - Hispanic or Latino

Hispanic or Latino ethnicity has seen a steady increase over the past 20 years and stood at 15.2% in 2020. The distribution of Hispanic or Latino shows a distinct geographic pattern.

Character

Race/Ethnicity - White or Caucasian

White or Caucasian continues a decline in population percentage, dropping from 61% in 2000 to 44.7% in 2020 as Mecklenburg County becomes more diverse.

As of 2020, 55.3% of Mecklenburg County residents were people of color, compared to just 49.4% of the population in 2010. The distribution shows a distinct geographic pattern.

Economy

Job Density

Job density continues to increase in Mecklenburg County, oriented primarily along major transportation corridors. 95.2% of adults in the labor force were employed in 2020.

As of 2019, there were 700,467 jobs in Mecklenburg County, 73.7% more than in 2002. 50.7% of jobs are held by workers who commute in from another county.

Economy

Commercial Space

While commercial space increased to 464M SqFt in 2020, the average square footage space per parcel has dropped considerably since 2017 as commercial space becomes more disaggregated in the County.

Economy

High Speed Internet Access

In 2020, 79% of Mecklenburg County residents have access to high speed Internet, with a corridor below I-85 and above downtown Charlotte with the least access despite having generally high population density.

Roughly 110,000 people in Mecklenburg County have no Internet access.

Education

Education Level - Bachelor's Degree

45.8% of adults over the age of 25 have a bachelor's degree or higher. Mecklenburg County is significantly higher than North Carolina as a whole (28%), but ranked 28th among large metro areas in a 2012 study.

The county shows a distinct band of lower performance, which occurs on a number of education metrics.

Education

Test Proficiency - Elementary School

Proficiency in End of Grade testing in elementary school has remained relatively steady since 2014 and is 48.1% in 2019. EOG testing results show a distinct geographic distribution throughout the County.

Education

Student Absenteeism

13.5% of CMS students were absent 10 or more days during 2019, and increase from 8.2% in 2013. Absenteeism varies greatly over the county, with some neighborhood profile areas exceeding 30%.

Only a handful of states collect data on chronic absenteeism, but results from a sample of states suggest that an estimated 10-15% of students in the U.S. are chronically absent each year.

Engagement

311 Requests

Mecklenburg County has seen an increase in 311 requests, averaging 32 calls per 100 people in 2021. 311 handled 355,490 requests in 2021, a 46.4% increase in just five years.

Environment

Water Consumption

Average single-family household daily water consumption decreased from 177 gallons per day in 2010 to 152 gallons per day in 2020.

Lawn watering and landscape irrigation is estimated to account for nearly one-third of all residential water use.

Environment

Residential Solid Waste

The average daily waste generation per household rose sharply in 2021 to 9 pounds per day. Residences generated 1,864,894 pounds of solid waste per day in 2021.

Environment

Residential Solid Waste Diversion

Mecklenburg County saw a sharp decline in the percentage of residential solid waste diverted from landfills through recycling and yard waste processing, dropping from 35.8% in 2013 to 25.8% in 2021. The Environmental Protection Agency estimates that 75% of solid waste is recyclable.

Health

Prenatal Care

The percentage of births where the mother received adequate pre-natal care continues a slow decline in Mecklenburg. While 71.9% received adequate pre-natal care in 2012, only 57.6% received adequate care in 2019.

Conversely, the infant death rate has declined steadily over the same time period. Infant death rates vary greatly by race. African American infants are five times more likely to die before their first birthday than are White infants.

Health

Proximity to Public Outdoor Recreation

Proximity to outdoor recreation continues to improve, with 58.4% of housing units within 1/2 mile of and outdoor public recreation area in 2020. Similar trends can be seen in the proximity to pharmacies and grocery stores. The geographic distribution of such access is not ubiquitous, however.

Health

Proximity to Low-Cost Health Care

The percentage of housing units within 1/2 mile of a Medicaid provider or free clinic saw an improvement after a slow deline from 2016 to 2018, measuring at 29.6% in 2019. The geographic distribution, however, is uneven.

Housing

House Sales Price

Mecklenburg County, like much of the United States, has seen a sharp increase in the average sale price of homes, averaging $365,860 in 2021. The National Association of Realtors reports that Mecklenburg County home prices rose by 22.4% from 2020 to 2021. The real estate market has been cooling toward the end of 2022, however.

Housing

Residential New Construction

The amount and concentration of residential units permitted for new construction has oscilated in recent years, ending with 4 units per acre and 12,751 new units in 2021. According to a 2021 report by the Joint Center for Housing Studies, single family housing starts are up by 13.2%.

Housing

Single-Family Housing

The percentage of single family housing units continues a slow decline, ending at 55.7% in 2021.

Safety

Crime - Property

Many safety metrics, including property crimes, disorder related calls, and nuisance violations have declined in recent years. Mecklenburg averaged 31 property crimes per 1,000 residents in 2021. The Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police Department reported 33,094 property offenses overall in calendar year 2020, a 14% decrease from 2019. The number of residential burglaries were down 31%, commercial burgularies were down 6%, and vehicle break-ins decreased by 14%.

Safety

Crime - Violent

Violent crime has increased in recent years, averaging 5 crimes per 1,000 residents in 2021. The Charlotte-Mecklenburg Police Department reported 4,981 violent offenses overall in calendar year 2020, 16% higher than in 2019. The number of homicides was 18% higher, rape was 17% higher, robbery was 8% higher, and aggravated assault was 29% higher.

Transportation

Transit Ridership

Transit ridership saw a sharp decline in 2021 due to the pandemic, dropping to 42 average weekly bus and train boardings per stop. In 2019, 2.9% of commuters who live in Mecklenburg County traveled regularly by public transportation, compared to 5% nationally.

Transportation

Long Commute

In 2020, 60% of auto commuters averaged traveling 20 minutes or more to work. Mecklenburg had a higher percentage of long commutes than North Carolina (55%) or the nation (57%). According to the 2021 INRIX Traffic Scorecard, drivers in the Charlotte metro area waste 17 hours commuting per year.

Additional Resources
